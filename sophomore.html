True Grit

In True Grit, we were tasked with creating a game for middle school students that demonstrates the importance of teamwork, cooperation, and foresight. My team did this with an engaging card game that has you stranded in a cold taiga with no hope of rescue. You and your fellow players have to strategize, and cooperate to survive. I realized that it is very difficult to create a game that rewards people for their cooperation and punishes them for abandoning their teammates in a way that can be played in a few minutes. I think we did a decent job. Our game didn't do well in rewarding teamwork (i blame the core idea being too simple to simulate that), but we rewarded foresight very well. The game is based on making desicions to change the outcome of an event, so if it didn't reward forsight, we'd need to go to back to the drawing board.

During the project, I focused my efforts on improving my communication and planning. Communication was an important skill in this project, I ended up leading the team, and proper communication and delegation led to having a working project by the due date. To tie into what we learned in classes, In math we learned about probability to better understand how many descisons can be made in a flowchart of desicions. We created game assets and a website for art, and in English and Debate, we looked at how real people went through hardships to better create our games and make them emulate the intricacies of teamwork and foresight.

Really, Really, Cold Case

Really, Really Cold Case was a project that had us create a plausible murder case file for a notable historical figure. My team and I chose to kill Karl Marx. This involved researching Karl Marx's life and find out where he lived, when he lived, and who would want him dead. After we had determined who would kill him, we pieced together an intricate case file with the evidence we had created. We even modeled a 3D printed knife using CAD! You can find the detailed case file here.

During this project, I took more of a following role in team decisions. I improved the most in my communication role, as I created the website using as little words, and as clear sentences as possible. I didn't want the main outlet of information for our case file to be disorganised mess.

During the project, we learned many practical things that contributed to out project. We learned about logarithms and the laws of cooling in Algebra 2. This obviously contributed a lot of credibility to our evidence. We could prove when the victim was killed based on his body temperature. We looked at the logic behind proving things with mathematical proofs. We asked, "Why?" to everything during the project, and I beleive that added hugely to our Investigation skills. Beneath the Surface

In Beneath the Surface we were taking the role of a country in the world and doing 2 things, designing propaganda for our "citizens" and building a submarine to gather "intelligence" from our enemies. My team chose to become Israel, so the propaganda we designed was for Israeli citzens. We created a campaign that could've run during the Arab-Israeli War of 1948. It was a short war that occured after the British Mandate over palenstine expired.

To complete the submarine portion of the project, the whole class created a game we could play with the subs in a pool. We had to gather magnets from objects in the water. These included a magnet at the bottom of a bucket, a bouy held down my a magnet, and more. My team was tasked with designing a submarine capable of getting the magnet out the bucket. Our design had 4 arms that would guide the sub's nose to the center of the bucket, and 3 motors to maneuver. 1 for rotating the sub up and down, and 2 for forwards/backwards/left/right.

This project was incrdibly intense in communication and investigation skills, because, as enemies, we couldn't let our secrets be known by the other teams. We created encryption systems using functions (and their inverse function for decryption), and basic computer science like the structure of binary letters in different encoding methods like ASCII or unicode. Not only were communication and investigation important for encryption, but they were also important for being able to create convincing propaganda and a reliable submarine. These skills were incredibly important for understanding how people would react and how to best troubleshoot a submarine.

In Quantum Leap, we were tasked with building a video game that brought the players through different human times and cultures with a narrative and art style. From start to finish, the project took 2 months. You can check it out here. It was a really good learning experience for time management and project management. We lived and breathed Hofstader's Law, but in the end, we had implemented enough of our initial scope to have an effective demo. Computer Games are not simple things to produce. Physics had to move the characters around the world, art put the characters on the screen from our ideas, and history and narrative design were ingrained the in the story of our world.
